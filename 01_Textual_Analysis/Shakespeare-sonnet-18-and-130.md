# Sonnet 18

## The Question: Can human possess "eternal summer"?

From texts in Sonnet 18, Shakespeare said “your eternal summer”. How is that possible? Because humans can’t be immortal. 

## A Probable Interpretation

Shakespeare used the word “eternal lines” in the third-to-last line, so the eternal one is not the lady Shakespeare referred to, but the lady housed in Shakespeare’s poems. Lady housed in poems is not a real person, I mean, the prototype might be a real person. But the lady who has “eternal summer” is the impression that will appear in people’s minds. Since it’s an impression, it is not “someone”.

Perhaps he was not praising a specific person, but merely creating a universal perception of beauty.

## Observation Might Support My Interpretation: No Detailed Description of the Lady

There is no detailed description in the poetry. If Shakespeare wanted to introduce a lady to readers, why wouldn’t he describe some details exclusive to this lady to distinguish her from a typical perception that suits everyone?

## Summer's Day: Is it an intentional ambiguity?

I’ve noticed the metaphor “summer’s day”. Intuitively, “summer’s day” to 17th century British was perhaps a common perception of beauty. Since rainy days were the normalcy. If this was true, it'll enhance my interpretation: this metaphor serves for creating a universal impression popped into the minds of 17th century British readers, Shakespeare’s intended readers, instead of introducing a real person.
